### Why a custom map print is the gift they'll keep forever

There's a particular kind of gift that stops someone mid-unwrap.

Not the expensive kind. Not the surprising kind. The specific kind — the one that makes it obvious you were actually paying attention.

A personalised map print can be that gift. Not because it's rare or complicated to arrange. But because it's about a place. And places, for most people, carry more weight than almost anything else.

The street where they grew up. The city they left behind. The spot by the harbour where everything changed one ordinary Tuesday afternoon. When you give someone a beautifully printed map of that place — framed, considered, made for their wall — what you're really saying is: I know what matters to you. I remembered.

That's hard to buy. It's not hard to make.

#### Why place holds so much meaning

Psychologists have a term for it: place attachment. The emotional bonds people form with specific locations aren't incidental. They're part of how we understand ourselves and our history.

Think about the places that defined you. The childhood bedroom window with its view of the same roof for years. The city you moved to at 22 when anything still felt possible. The mountain you climbed together the summer before everything got complicated.

Those places don't leave you. They become part of the furniture of your memory — present in ways you'd never quite be able to explain to someone who wasn't there.

A map acknowledges that. It says: this place was real. It mattered. Here it is, rendered in detail you can run your fingers over. Art you can live with.

#### What makes a map print different from any other personalised gift

Personalised gifts occupy a strange middle ground. Done well, they're irreplaceable. Done badly — with a generic font on a mug, a stock photograph with a name dropped in — they feel like an afterthought disguised as consideration.

The difference is specificity. The more specific the object, the more it communicates genuine attention.

A map of someone's hometown is specific in a way that a "personalised" journal isn't. A map of the town square in the village where they got married is almost impossibly so. Every cartographic line is telling the truth about a real place that exists, or existed, in that person's life.

That's the thing about maps as art: they can't be faked. The geography is real. The place is real. And so is the feeling it carries.

#### Choosing the right map style for the person

Not all map art should look the same. The style of the print should reflect who it's for — their aesthetic, their space, the feeling you want the gift to carry.

**For the person with a sleek, contemporary flat**: the Modern style — clean lines, high contrast, built for city apartments.

**For the person who loves a statement piece**: the Slate style — dark background, precise cartographic detail, designed to command a wall.

**For the traditionalist with a study full of atlases**: the Classic style — warm, cartographic, rooted in the aesthetic of exploration.

**For the one who spends every winter on the mountain**: the Ski style — terrain-focused, made for alpine locations.

**For the nature lover with a home full of plants and wood**: the Mineral or Terrain styles — earthy, organic, honest.

The customiser lets you see every style applied to your chosen location before you commit to anything. Take the time to look. The right one will be obvious.

#### Getting the details right

A few practical things to consider before you order:

**The location**: Be as specific as you like. You can zoom into a single street, centre on a neighbourhood, or pull out to show the whole coastline. The customiser gives you full control. Decide what the map is really *of* — the building, the street, the town, the whole region.

**The text**: Most meaningful map prints include a small amount of text — a date, a set of coordinates, a place name, a short phrase. Keep it simple. The fewer words, the more considered it feels.

**The size**: Think about the wall it's going on. A small print on a large wall gets lost. A large print in a small space can feel overwhelming. Our sizing guide walks you through scale and proportion if you're unsure.

**The frame**: CoolMaps prints are designed to be frame-ready. If you're ordering a gift, consider whether you're framing it before giving, or whether you want to leave that choice to the recipient.

#### The places worth printing

Some of the most moving maps we've seen ordered are not of famous cities. They're of small places — unremarkable to anyone on the outside, irreplaceable to the person who knows them.

A village in the Alentejo. A stretch of Scottish coastline that nobody visits. A suburb of a mid-sized city where someone spent their entire childhood and then left.

These places are not on anyone's bucket list. But they're on someone's heart. And that's exactly where a map print belongs.

### How to use map wall art in your home (and get it right)

A map on a wall is one of those interior choices that works harder than it looks.

It carries place. It carries memory. It adds topographic texture and cartographic line work that no other category of art quite replicates. Done well, it becomes one of those things people comment on when they visit for the first time.

Done badly — wrong size, wrong style, wrong wall — it disappears into the background, or worse, looks like a prop.

Here's how to get it right.

#### Start with the room, not the map

Before you choose a style or a location, look at the room itself.

What's the dominant palette? Warm tones — terracotta, oak, linen — call for something earthier: the Mineral or Paper styles, or a Classic print in warm cartographic colours. Cool tones — white walls, grey furniture, concrete surfaces — pair better with Modern or Slate.

What's the ceiling height? Higher ceilings can carry a larger print or a vertical format. Lower ceilings tend to suit a horizontal or square orientation.

What's already on the walls? A map print works as a standalone centrepiece, or as the anchor of a gallery wall. Both are valid — but the sizing and framing choices change significantly.

Spend five minutes with the room before you open the customiser. The right style usually becomes obvious once you're actually looking.

#### The eight styles and where they work

**Modern** is the most versatile. Clean, high contrast, no clutter — it disappears into contemporary spaces and looks deliberately chosen in minimal interiors. Works particularly well for city maps.

**Slate** is the statement choice. A dark background with precise cartographic detail: it commands attention. Best used as a standalone piece on a feature wall, not buried in a gallery arrangement.

**Classic** belongs in rooms with character — a study, a library, a room with original features or heritage furniture. The warm cartographic tones feel at home with books and wood.

**Ski** is purpose-built for alpine locations. If you're mapping a ski resort or mountain holiday, there's nothing better. In a non-mountain location, the style can feel misapplied.

**Terrain** shows the land at its most honest — elevation, relief, the texture of the earth. Works well for rural areas, national parks, coastal cliffs. In a room with natural materials (wood, linen, stone), it feels like it belongs.

**Paper** has nostalgia built in. An aged, warm tone that reads like a fragment from an old atlas. Pairs well with vintage furniture, travel photography, and interiors that value the patina of time.

**Blue** is the coastal choice. Ocean and water tones dominate — it suits seaside locations and nautical interiors naturally. In a landlocked city apartment, it can feel disconnected.

**Mineral** is the earthy warmth option — terracotta, sage, muted tones. Suits homes decorated with natural materials, plants, warm lighting. The Mineral style is CoolMaps' most distinctive aesthetic.

#### Sizing: the most common mistake

Most people order too small.

A print that looks substantial on a product page can look small on a real wall. The reason: we're used to seeing artwork on screens at a scale that doesn't translate.

A rough guide:

- **A3 (29.7 × 42 cm)**: Suits a small wall, a shelf, or part of a gallery arrangement
- **A2 (42 × 59.4 cm)**: The most versatile size — works as a centrepiece on most walls
- **A1 (59.4 × 84.1 cm)**: Makes a genuine statement; needs a substantial wall and a good frame
- **50 × 70 cm**: A common print standard; sits between A2 and A1 in impact

If in doubt, go up a size. You can always reduce the visual weight with a thin, understated frame. You can't make a small print fill a wall.

#### Framing considerations

CoolMaps prints are designed to be frame-ready — the border and proportions are set up for standard frame sizes.

For most interiors, a simple frame in black, white, natural oak, or warm walnut will work. Match the frame to the dominant material in the room, not to the print itself.

A mat (mount board) between print and frame adds depth and makes the whole thing feel more considered. It's a small addition that makes a significant visual difference.

If you're creating a gallery wall, keep frames consistent — same colour, same profile, same width. The map print will anchor it. Let the consistency of the frames do the compositional work.

#### The location question

Interior design considerations aside, the most important question for any map print is: *which place?*

The answer is always the one that means something. The right place for your map is never "a nice city" or "somewhere interesting." It's the place that has a story attached to it — personal, specific, yours.

That's what makes map art different from any other kind of wall art. The aesthetic can be beautiful. But the meaning is irreplaceable.
